Write Up

Rationale for our design decisions:

We used a calming teal and green color theme to match the healthcare focus of our project and make the interface feel clean and trustworthy. The buttons, dropdown, and info cards are styled with soft corners, clear spacing, and gentle shadows to keep everything organized and easy to read. We kept the layout simple and consistent so users can focus on the patient data without distractions. Interactive elements like buttons and tooltips respond smoothly to user actions, and the text is clear and readable on all screen sizes. Overall, the design supports a professional and caring experience for viewing medical information.

How did we choose our particular visual encodings and interaction techniques?

Since we're working with time series data, we wanted to make it easy for users to explore glucose trends over time. To do that, we used a line graph as our main visual, mapping time to the x-axis and glucose levels to the y-axis. This layout puts all data on a consistent scale and helps users quickly spot high or low glucose values and see when they occurred. To make the chart more interactive, we added a vertical tracking bar and tooltip that follows the user's cursor, so as they scrub through the graph, they can instantly see the exact time and glucose reading at any point. We also overlaid semi-transparent dots along the line to mark meal times. These dots vary in size to reflect the amount of sugar in each meal, and hovering over them reveals details like the food items eaten and their sugar content. This gives users a clearer picture of the patient's diet and how different meals may be impacting glucose levels. The main goal of our visualization is to help viewers understand how a patient's HBA1c, their diabetic status, affects their blood glucose levels when they consume a specific food. Finally, we included a zoom feature that lets users take a closer look at specific glucose events during a particular time period.

What alternatives did we consider and how did we arrive at our ultimate choices?

We considered using a brush selector to allow users to select multiple food events at once, but ultimately decided against it. We felt it added unnecessary visual clutter and didn't align well with our goal of keeping the interface clean and intuitive. We also explored using a brush tool for zooming, but instead opted for a simpler approach that lets users zoom in and out with their default scroll wheel. This method felt more responsive and familiar, providing a smoother experience when exploring the data. We wanted to include clear distinctions between these spikes and the time they a food item was consumed, we did think about doing bars to delineate when a food was consumed but since our main point relied less on food consumption and more on the level of the spike, we decided a dot would be sufficient.

An overview of our development process

We kicked things off by meeting as a group to browse datasets from PhysioNet, looking for one we were all genuinely interested in. Each of us picked a few datasets and did some initial exploratory data analysis (EDA) on our own using Python. After that, we regrouped to share our findings, ideas, and visualizations. In the end, we agreed to use the Glycemic Variability and Wearable Device Data from the BIG IDEAs Lab. Once we settled on the dataset, we started brainstorming how the visualization should look and how users would interact with it. Each group member sketched out ideas, and we came to a shared design direction that everyone liked. From there, we met again to divide the work evenly and set up a timeline with clear goals. Once development started, our workflow looked something like this:

  • Cleaned and formatted the data using Pandas
  • Built a line graph for individual patient data
  • Created a line graph for aggregate patient trends
  • Implemented core button functionalities
  • Started styling the page
  • Added interactions like line selection, tooltips, and zoom
  • Added patient info display
  • Wrote contextual info explaining glucose metrics
  • Did a lot of styling tweaks and layout refinements
  • Improved interaction behavior and responsiveness
  • Built the write-up/info page
  • Finalized the overall layout and design

Throughout the process, we tried to stay flexible, adjusting based on what was working and what wasn't, and always aimed to keep the user experience clean and intuitive.
