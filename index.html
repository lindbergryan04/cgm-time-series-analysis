Understanding Metrics

HbA1c: The HbA1c test measures a patient's average blood sugar levels over the past two to three months. It's commonly used to diagnose diabetes and assess the risk of developing it.

Hyperglycemia: Hyperglycemia refers to having too much glucose in the blood. A person is considered prediabetic if their levels fall between 140â€“200 mg/dL one to two hours after eating. We chose 170 mg/dL as the hyperglycemia threshold in our graph, as it sits near the midpoint of this range.

Hypoglycemia: Hypoglycemia occurs when blood sugar drops below normal levels â€” typically anything under 70 mg/dL.

Prediabetes: A person is considered prediabetic if their HbA1c is between 5.7% and 6.4%. A score of 6.5% or higher is considered diabetic. People with prediabetes tend to spend more time in a hyperglycemic state compared to those without it. Extended time in this range increases the risk of developing type 2 diabetes and other complications.

Conclusion

Individuals with prediabetes tend to experience sharper spikes in glucose levels following food consumption and generally spend more time in hyperglycemic ranges compared to those without prediabetes. This pattern reflects impaired glucose regulation. However, it's important to note that discrepancies in the data may arise due to the short 10-day monitoring window. Random fluctuations in diet can influence glucose patterns.
